Example 5.3.11.

Consider the graph in Figure 5.3.12. Suppose the vertices represent stations in a factory, and the edges represent conveyor belts between the stations.

A manager in the factory may be interested in how efficiently the conveyor belts can be run. Suppose that belt \(cd\) costs $11/day to run, belt \(de\) costs $8/day to run, belt \(cf\) costs $9/day to run, belt \(df\) costs $12/day to run, and belt \(ef\) costs $11/day to run. We can represent this by labeling each edge with its cost, as seen in Figure 5.3.13.

This is an example of a weighted graph, which is just a graph with the edges labeled. The labels usually represent something meaningful about the situation the graph represents. For example, we could have weighted the edges in Figure 5.3.13 with the time it takes to move an object from one station to another, or the distance between stations (as one might find on a map), etc.
